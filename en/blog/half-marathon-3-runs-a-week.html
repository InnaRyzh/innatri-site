The 3-Day-a-Week Half Marathon Plan for Runners With a Job, a Family, and a Life

Inna Ryzhykh • 2026-07-21 • 7 MIN READ

Want to know the question I hear most as a coach? It's not about pace. It's not about heart rate.

"Inna, how many days a week do I HAVE to run?"

And I can hear what's really behind it. The job. The kids. The tiredness. And that quiet guilt: real runners train five days a week, and here I am with my three, barely a runner at all…

So let me say it plainly. You can train for a half marathon on three runs a week. Properly train, personal-record-level train. That's not me being a nice coach. That's data.

What the research says: the FIRST study

Here's the part I love. The FIRST lab at Furman University took a group of recreational runners and stripped their week down to three key runs, plus optional easy cross-training. Runner's World covered the results back in 2004, and they still annoy the "more miles, always" crowd:

Lactate threshold speed improved by 2.3% on top. And here's the kicker: the researchers had expected to lose at least five runners to injuries. The opposite happened - fewer running days meant less accumulated impact, which meant healthier legs. If your running history includes injuries that showed up out of nowhere, that might be the strongest argument on this page. By the way, I've written a full breakdown of why knees break from loading errors, not from running: is running bad for your knees.

But there's a condition. And it's non-negotiable.

Three runs only work when every run has a job

This is the whole difference. Five sluggish "whatever pace feels okay" runs lose to three precise ones, because a 3-day plan has no junk miles. Each run solves one problem:

1. Intervals. Short fast repeats with recovery between them. They raise your VO2max, your engine's ceiling. This is the run that leaves you lying in the grass, wondering why you do this to yourself. That's the one that makes you faster.

2. Tempo. Twenty to forty minutes at "uncomfortable but sustainable" effort. It teaches your body to clear lactate and teaches your head to stay calm when it gets hard. For the half marathon this is the money workout: the race happens in exactly this zone.

3. The long run. Sixty to 120 minutes of slow running. It builds your aerobic base and toughens tendons and ligaments. Slow means actually slow, by heart rate, not "a little quicker today because I feel great." I broke down how to control that in Running by Heart Rate.

And the other four days? Recovery. Those are not empty days. A workout breaks you down; you get stronger afterward. While you sleep. While you eat. While your body rebuilds itself. On a 3-day plan, recovery finally gets the space it deserves.

Shuffle the days to fit your schedule. Keep one rule: at least one non-running day between runs.

Who this format is NOT for

Honestly, no sales pitch:

  • If you're starting from zero. Spend a few weeks alternating walking and running first. Intervals on unprepared legs are an injury application form.
  • If you're chasing an aggressive PR at your limit. Big ambitions eventually need more volume than three days can hold.
  • If "fewer days" turns into "every run at war." Three runs a week is not three races a week. Easy stays easy.

How many weeks you need to train for a half marathon

Short version: 12 weeks minimum. Sixteen is better. Here's why I insist on the buffer.

  • You run regularly and comfortably hold 40–60 minutes non-stop. Then 12 weeks is a working minimum for your first half.
  • You can run about 30 minutes without stopping. Plan on 16 weeks: the first chunk goes to building your base calmly.
  • You're starting from zero. The half marathon is not a "three months from now" goal - it's a six-months-from-now goal: 2–3 months of walk-run first, then a full 12–16-week cycle.

Why the buffer matters so much. Novice runners get injured more than twice as often as experienced recreational runners: a 2015 meta-analysis counted 17.8 injuries per 1,000 hours of running versus 7.7. And behind it, almost always, is the same thing - trying to squeeze a training block into a calendar that's too short. Rushing the plan is more dangerous than moving the race.

And the big one - health. This plan is written for healthy adults. If you have a heart or vascular condition, diabetes, joint problems, significant excess weight, if you're pregnant - or you're 40+ and haven't trained in years - see a doctor first, ideally with an exercise stress test (ECG under load). And one iron rule for every workout: chest pain, dizziness, unusually severe breathlessness - that's not something to push through. That's stop, and see a doctor.

FAQ

How long do you need to train for a half marathon?
A minimum of 12 weeks if you already run regularly and comfortably hold 40–60 minutes non-stop; 16 weeks is more comfortable. Complete beginners should plan on about six months: 2–3 months of walk-run first, then a full training cycle. Shortening the calendar is more dangerous than moving the race: novice runners get injured more than twice as often as experienced recreational runners.
Can you really finish a half marathon training 3 days a week?
Yes. The condition is structure: intervals + tempo + long run, not "three runs however they happen." The FIRST study showed recreational runners on this format didn't just finish, they set PRs.
What if I miss a run?
Nothing. You don't make it up. You move on with the plan. Missed runs aren't dangerous; cramming two hard runs into two consecutive days is.
Do I need cross-training on rest days?
It helps, but it's optional. Thirty minutes of cycling, swimming, or brisk walking speeds up recovery and adds aerobic volume with zero impact. If the choice is cross-training or sleep, choose sleep.
What about strength work?
Yes. One or two short strength sessions a week meaningfully cut injury risk. Put them on running days, in the evening.

To sum it up: the question was never how many days a week you run. The question is whether every run you do has a job.

You get out what you put in. Not how many times you showed up, but what you showed up to do.
